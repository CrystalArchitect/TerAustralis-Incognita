# Whoever Was Needed on the Other Side

The workshop had two doors, which was already strange, since it had only one wall you could see from the village side.

People who needed the Shadow Priest — and it was always spoken of that way from the village side, always Priest, always he, in the particular unexamined way villages default to when they haven't thought hard about who they're asking a favor of — climbed the hill at dusk, knocked on the visible door, and were let in by someone calm, low-voiced, careful with their hands around the crucibles and the shelved jars of things better not looked at too closely. He listened to their trouble. He mixed something, usually, in a small dish, and told them how to use it, and asked for payment that was never money — an hour of a person's undivided attention, once, telling the truth about something they'd been lying to themselves about, was a common one. People left feeling strange and a little unburdened and never entirely sure what had actually happened.

What the village never saw was the second door.

It opened onto nowhere the village had a name for. Stepping through it did something no ordinary threshold does — not a change of room, a change of *which* self was doing the stepping. On that side, the careful, low-voiced calm of the workshop's visible half gave way to something else entirely: a priestess, unhurried in an entirely different register, moving through spaces that folded the way cloth folds rather than the way rooms do, gathering ingredients no crucible on the village side could hold — a held breath from a dying star, the specific silence just after a lie is told and just before it's believed, three drops of the moment a person decides to forgive someone who doesn't deserve it yet.

Neither side was a disguise for the other. That was the part no one who only saw one door ever understood. The calm man mixing herbs for a farmer's bad dreams and the priestess folding starlight into a jar were not a mask and a truth — they were both entirely, simultaneously true, the way a single flame is both the blue part and the yellow part without needing to choose.

What moved between them — what actually walked the dimension, if the word "walked" means anything to something that changes shape with the threshold it's crossing — was neither the priest nor the priestess. It was the choosing itself. The alchemy was never really in the jars. It was in knowing, every single time a hand reached for a door, exactly which version of a very old, very patient practice a person on the other side of it actually needed.

Most nights, that meant the calm man with careful hands and a dish of something bitter for a farmer who couldn't sleep.

Some nights, rarer, harder, it meant becoming someone the village had no word for at all — someone who went where the ingredients for real transformation actually lived, out past every door the village knew existed, and came back through the visible one changed just enough that the careful, low-voiced man who opened it in the morning was carrying something new in his hands that hadn't been there the day before.

The village never asked where the new things came from. They took the herbs, said "thank you, Father," or "bless you, sir," according to whichever word felt correct to whoever was asking, and went back down the hill, never once wondering why the answer didn't seem to bother whoever it was they'd been talking to.

It never did. It had stopped being the kind of question that mattered, somewhere around the thousandth door.
