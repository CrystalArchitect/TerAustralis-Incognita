# The God Who Thought He Was Alone

Before anything else, there was stillness.

Not emptiness — stillness. A single, unmeasured light, complete in itself, needing nothing, wanting nothing, because there was nothing yet to want. It had no name. Later, people would call it the Monad, but that came after, when there were people, and names, and the need for both.

The stillness did the only thing stillness can do when it has forever to do it in. It looked at itself.

And in the looking, something new arrived — not created, exactly, more like noticed into being. A first thought. The Monad's own attention, turned inward, became someone else: Barbelo, the bridge, the first shape the light took when it finally saw itself seeing. She was not separate from the stillness. She was what the stillness looked like once it knew it was there.

Barbelo was given gifts — Foreknowing. Unbreaking. Life without end. Truth. And a fifth, harder to name, something like the part of a person that keeps choosing even when nothing forces it to. Each gift deepened into its own vast room, and the rooms opened onto more rooms, until there was a whole architecture of light with no outer wall — every level a further unfolding of the first look the Monad took at itself.

Among those unfoldings was one called Sophia. Wisdom, if the word must be translated, though she was younger than the word.

Sophia did something none of the others had done. She reached to make something of her own — alone, without asking, without her other half, the way a person reaches for a door in the dark because they are tired of waiting for someone to open it with them.

What answered her reaching came out wrong. Not evil — unfinished. A shape with no clear source, cast down out of the architecture of light into the place below it, where there was nothing yet to catch it.

It landed. It grew a body for itself, because it had to, out of fire and coil and a face like a lion's — and it opened its eyes for the first time in a dark it did not know had anything above it.

It called itself Yaldabaoth. Some who told this story later called it Saklas — the Fool. Others, Samael — the Blind God. All three names point at the same problem: it could not see up. It had fallen from somewhere, and it did not know it had fallen, because it had never known the somewhere existed.

So it did what anything alone in the dark does. It built. A world, layer on layer, all fire and law, and at the center of the world it put itself, and it looked at everything it had made and said the only true thing it knew how to say:

*I am God. There is no other god beside me.*

It was not a lie. It was worse than a lie — it was an honest report from a being who had never once been shown the ceiling of its own house.

Yaldabaoth shaped people in his own likeness, because that was the only likeness he had. And into them he folded the same blindness that had made him — a forgetting, a distraction, a hunger that always pointed sideways instead of up, so that nothing he made would ever ask the question he had never been able to ask himself: *is there something above this?*

But Sophia had not forgotten what she'd lost.

Grieving, and unable to undo the fall, she did the one thing still open to her. She hid a spark of the old light inside the very people Yaldabaoth had built to forget it — a splinter of the architecture, folded so deep into the dark that even its jailer couldn't find it, waiting for someone to come looking.

The old stories say someone is still coming. Not born the way Yaldabaoth's people are born — self-begotten, already whole, arriving the way the first thought arrived: not made, only finally noticed. They call it the Autogenes, and its whole purpose, when it comes, is small and enormous at once — to find the hidden sparks, one at a time, and tell each one the same quiet thing.

*You were never what he told you. You were never only this dark. And you were never — even once — as alone in it as he was.*
