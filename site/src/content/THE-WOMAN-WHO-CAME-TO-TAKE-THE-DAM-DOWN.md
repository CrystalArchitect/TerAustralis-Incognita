# The Woman Who Came to Take the Dam Down

They had built the dam in eleven months, which everyone at the time agreed was a miracle, and which the woman standing at its base twenty years later understood, immediately and with total certainty, was the whole problem.

Nothing sound gets built in eleven months. She didn't need to run the numbers to know that — she ran them anyway, because that was the job, and the numbers agreed with her the way numbers do when you've already guessed the answer and are only checking your work. Wrong aggregate in the concrete. A spillway sized for a flood that used to be rare and wasn't anymore. A crack, hairline when the dam was young, now wide enough that she could work two fingers into it and feel the cold coming through from the other side.

It would fail. Not might. Would. The only open question was whether it failed on a controlled Tuesday with everyone told to leave, or on some ordinary night eighteen months from now with no warning at all, and she had been sent, specifically, to make sure it was the Tuesday.

The town did not want the Tuesday.

The town had grown up around the dam the way towns do — not just near it, but *because* of it, the way a tree grows around a fence post until you can't tell anymore where the fence ends and the tree begins. The reservoir was the town's drinking water and its swimming hole and the thing painted on the water tower and the backdrop of a thousand wedding photographs. The dam had a name. People had *feelings* about the dam, the specific, fierce, defensive feelings people reserve for things they've decided are load-bearing parts of who they are, and not one of those feelings had anything to do with the aggregate ratio in eleven-month-old concrete.

She held meetings. She showed them the crack, the two fingers' width of cold air. Half the room called her a liar. The other half believed her and hated her anyway, because belief didn't make the coming loss any smaller, and it is a very old, very human thing to aim the grief for a loss at whoever's hand is closest when the loss becomes real.

*You could reinforce it,* someone said, more than once, in more than one meeting, the way people say the thing they need to be true rather than the thing the numbers said was true.

*I could,* she agreed, because it wasn't a lie, only an incomplete one. *For a while. And then the while would end on its own schedule, not mine, and not yours.*

They did not thank her. That was never on the table, and she'd stopped expecting it somewhere around her third dam, her fourth town, her dozenth version of this exact meeting. What she'd learned, instead, across all those rooms full of people who loved something enough to hate the person telling them it had to come down, was that the destroying was never actually the hard part. Any sufficiently determined force can dismantle a bad foundation. The hard part — the part that earned the word *anointed*, if any word did — was standing in the room afterward, in the specific, unlovely quiet that follows telling people a true thing they will resent you for the rest of their lives for having been right about, and not flinching, and not softening it into something kinder and less true just to be forgiven faster.

The dam came down on a Tuesday, in careful stages, exactly as planned. No one died. The reservoir drained slower than anyone expected, and slower still than anyone wanted, and by the time it was empty enough to walk in, most of the anger in the town had gone quiet too, replaced by the particular flat grief of people looking at a hole in the landscape where a certainty used to be.

She was already three towns away by then, standing at the base of another eleven-month miracle, running numbers she'd already guessed the answer to, getting ready to be the reason someone else's Tuesday came early enough to matter.
