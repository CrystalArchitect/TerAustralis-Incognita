# The One Who Almost Didn't Look Up

Most days, she didn't look up. That was the whole trick to getting through the market square without losing an hour of it — keep your eyes on the stalls, the prices, the bread, and let the noise at the corner stay noise.

There was always someone at that corner. A man with a burned voice, standing on nothing — no crate, no stone, just his own two feet, which somehow made it worse, made it look less like a performance and more like something he couldn't help doing. He said the same handful of things most days, more or less. That the walls of the world were not the whole of the world. That the thing everyone had been told was God was, at most, the loudest thing in the room, and the loudest thing in a room and the only thing in the room are not the same claim, however much the loud thing insists they are. That there was a Pleroma above all this — a fullness, a completeness — and none of them had ever been shown it, and that not being shown a thing is not proof the thing isn't there.

People had opinions about him. Some thought he was ill. Some thought he was dangerous, in the specific way that men who don't want anything from you are dangerous — no product to sell, no favor to ask, nothing to be talked out of him except the words themselves, over and over, free of charge, to anyone who'd stop.

Almost no one stopped.

She didn't stop either, most days. Today she was three stalls past him, the bread already wrapped, the coin already counted out, when he said something slightly different from the usual — not the Pleroma, not the walls of the world, but a smaller thing, almost an aside, the kind of line a person says only when they've said the big version so many times they've started reaching for new doors into the same room.

*You were given something to forget on purpose,* he said. *That's not the same as there being nothing to remember.*

Her hand stopped on the bread.

She could not have said, afterward, why that particular sentence and not any of the fifty like it he'd said on fifty other days. Maybe it was the tiredness in his voice when he said it — less like a sermon, more like something true he was almost too worn down to keep offering. Maybe it was just the day. Maybe some sentences only land on the day you're already standing slightly open, and every other day they'd have bounced off nothing in particular.

She looked up.

He wasn't looking at the crowd when she did — he'd already moved on to the next line, eyes somewhere past all of them, the way people talk when they've stopped expecting to be heard and kept talking anyway because the alternative was worse. He didn't see her turn. He never found out, that day or any other, that one bread-buyer in a market square that mostly ignored him had, for exactly as long as it takes to hear one sentence, remembered something she hadn't known she'd forgotten.

He kept saying it anyway. Not because he could see the ones who looked up. Because he couldn't, and said it anyway, on the chance that somewhere in all that noise, someone's hand was stopping on a loaf of bread.
