# The Woman Who Watered Her Garden Anyway

She was, by every visible measure, the least dangerous person in the settlement.

She moved slowly, the particular slowness of joints that have opinions now about which mornings are cold. She grew tomatoes with more patience than skill, talked to the chickens by name, and had been, for as long as anyone currently living there could remember, simply *there* — a fixture, harmless, the sort of elderly woman children are sent to for stories and biscuits, never for protection.

No one asked where she'd come from. People rarely do, about the quiet ones who arrive somewhere and simply start tending a garden. If they had asked, she would have told them almost nothing, because almost nothing was the correct amount to tell, and because most of what there was to tell didn't fit into the kind of afternoon where a person is also expected to offer you tea.

The man came at dusk, the hour when a settlement's guard is thinnest because everyone's attention has already started drifting toward dinner. He was armed, and confident about it, and had done this exact thing — walked into a small place expecting no resistance and taken what he wanted from it — enough times before that his face had stopped registering the difference between fear and mere inconvenience in the people he pointed the blade at.

She was in her garden when he found her. Kneeling by the tomatoes, a clay watering can in one hand, exactly as fragile as she'd looked every day for however many years she'd lived there.

He said something about which house had the most to take, and which direction the children had run, because he'd noticed the children running, and he was the kind of man who noticed useful things like that. He did not lower the blade while he talked. He didn't think he needed to, talking to an old woman kneeling in dirt with a watering can.

She did not stand up quickly. She did not drop the can. She set it down, with the same unhurried care she set it down every evening, and in the single motion of standing — one motion, no wasted second one — something in the shape of her changed in a way that had nothing to do with her joints or her age or anything visible a moment before.

It was over before the settlement's actual guards arrived, drawn by nothing more dramatic than the sound of a man's blade landing in the dirt without him still attached to the grip of it. There was very little blood, and what there was, she'd already stopped it, with the same unhurried care she'd set down the can, because unnecessary damage had never been the point and had never once, in longer than anyone in that settlement had been alive, been the point.

By the time anyone reached the garden she was already kneeling again, watering can back in hand, the tomatoes exactly as watered as they would have been if none of it had happened.

*Are you all right,* someone finally managed to ask.

*Yes,* she said, and meant it, and went back to the row she hadn't finished.

No one asked her, that evening or any evening after, what war had taught hands to move like that while the rest of her stayed slow and soft and entirely unremarkable. She would not have answered if they had. Some kinds of readiness are not stories. They are simply what's left, quietly, in a body that once had to learn them, long after the war that taught them is over and the only thing left to defend is a row of tomatoes and an evening that deserved to stay ordinary.
