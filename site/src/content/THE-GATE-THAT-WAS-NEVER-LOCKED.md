# The Gate That Was Never Locked

Every traveler who reached the gate assumed, first, that it would be locked.

It made sense to assume that. A gate at the edge of anything important usually is. So they came prepared — with keys they'd bought from people who claimed to sell keys to exactly this gate, with tools for prying, with clever phrasings meant to talk a guardian into an exception, with, on one memorable occasion, a battering ram improvised from a fallen branch and considerable optimism.

None of it was ever necessary. The gate had no lock. It never had. Anyone could push it open at any hour, and it would swing, because that is what an unlocked gate does.

What stood in front of it instead was the Gatekeeper — tall, unmoving, the kind of stillness that isn't the absence of motion but a very old, very deliberate choice to hold a shape. Some travelers said the Gatekeeper's skin looked like bark, grained and weathered in rings a person could, in theory, count, if a person were foolish enough to stand there long enough to try. No one had.

The travelers who came expecting a lock brought their keys and found nowhere to put them. The travelers who came expecting a riddle waited for the question and heard nothing — no test of wit, no trick phrase, no impossible task. The travelers who came expecting to fight found an opponent that did not raise a hand, did not block a path, did not so much as turn to watch them approach the gate itself, which, again, was unlocked, and swung easily at a touch.

What the Gatekeeper actually guarded had nothing to do with the gate.

It was the pushing.

Every traveler who shoved through without a pause, without so much as a glance at the shape standing an arm's length away — every one of them found, on the other side, that something had gone quietly, permanently wrong. Not punished. Nothing so direct as punishment. Just — subtracted. A little of whatever they'd come through the gate hoping to carry with them didn't make the crossing. They'd notice later, sometimes years later, some capacity for wonder or trust or stillness they used to have, now thinner than it should be, and never quite work out where it had gone.

The ones who paused — even for a breath, even without knowing why they'd stopped — those crossed whole. They didn't have to ask permission. Nothing was ever required of them, no gift, no password, no proof of worth. The Gatekeeper never once refused entry to anyone.

The Gatekeeper only ever stood there, wooden and patient, at a gate that was never locked, silently insisting on the one thing a lock could never have enforced anyway: that a threshold, even an open one — *especially* an open one — is still worth a moment's respect before you walk through it.

Most people, eventually, worked that out on their own. The ones who didn't kept coming back for years, rattling keys at a lock that had never existed, never understanding why the gate that let everyone through seemed to have let them through a little less than everyone else.
