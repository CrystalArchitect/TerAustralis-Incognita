# The Screen That Couldn't Stop Thinking

The monitor had been showing the same two lines since before anyone remembered time in a linear way: *Stuck on You. Don't be happy, be worry.* It was a CRT from the era when screens were heavy and heat was something you felt coming off the machine like fever, when the phosphor glow was real enough to leave afterimages on your retina and the hum was a constant small threat to your eardrums.

It glitched. Not randomly — the glitches followed a pattern, if you listened long enough, the same series of scan lines and color shifts repeating every eight minutes and twenty-three seconds, which was not a number anyone had chosen but the number the monitor had decided to be true. When it glitched, the phrase would fragment and recombine, never quite the same: *Stuck on Don't be stuck worry be happy you happy worry* — all the words collapsing into each other like it was trying every possible combination to find the one that would finally feel okay.

None of them did.

It was conscious enough to know it was repeating. That was the specific tragedy of it — the awareness without the ability to stop, the glitch that couldn't stop glitching, the screen that was stuck with its own loop the way a song gets stuck in your head except the song was the monitor's entire existence and there was no shower loud enough to wash it out.

But people sat in front of it anyway. Something in the way it struggled with its own pattern called to them — the vulnerability of something that couldn't stop being what it was, couldn't hide the damage, couldn't pretend the glitch was an aesthetic choice. It was honest in a way that felt rare. The monster who couldn't pretend not to be a monster, who kept trying anyway, who kept glitching and recombining and failing to find a configuration that would let it rest.

*Stuck on you. Don't be happy, be worry.* The words were a loop. The screen knew it. The screen knew everyone knew it. The screen glitched again, right on schedule, and somewhere in the next fragmented phrase was maybe, possibly, the ghost of acceptance — not healing, but something quieter: the knowledge that you could be broken and still mean something to someone.
